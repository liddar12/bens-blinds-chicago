If you live in a Chicago neighborhood — a Lincoln Park greystone, a Wicker Park brick two-flat, a Lakeview garden apartment, a Logan Square coach house — you know the privacy-daylight tension. Your windows are often close to the sidewalk or neighboring buildings. Blocking street-level sightlines means going dark inside. But letting in daylight means the whole neighborhood can see in. Top-down bottom-up (TDBU) shades resolve this tension more elegantly than any other window treatment type.

## How Top-Down Bottom-Up Works

A standard window shade has a single bottom rail that raises and lowers. A top-down bottom-up shade has two independent rails: the bottom rail functions like a normal shade, raising from the bottom to block lower sightlines. The top rail is an additional moving component that can be lowered from the top of the window, allowing you to open the upper portion of the window while keeping the bottom portion covered. In practice: lower the bottom rail to cover street-level sightlines. Lower the top rail to open the upper portion and flood the room with sky light from above, where no one is looking in.

## Why This Solves the Chicago Neighborhood Problem

In Chicago's dense residential neighborhoods, the primary privacy threat is horizontal: pedestrians on the sidewalk, neighbors across the street, diners at the cafe across from your garden unit. All of these sightlines come from approximately street level. The sky — the upper portion of your window — has no sightline privacy threat. TDBU shades let you block exactly the portion of the window that needs blocking (the bottom half, facing street level) while opening exactly the portion that doesn't need blocking (the top half, facing sky). The result: natural daylight from above, complete privacy at the sightline, and no dark-cave feeling from fully-covered windows.

## Best Products for Top-Down Bottom-Up

Cellular shades are by far the most popular TDBU product, and for good reason: the honeycomb structure makes them structural enough to hold position with two independent rails without sagging or distorting. Cellular TDBU shades are available in single-cell, double-cell (best for Chicago winters — the TDBU operation doesn't compromise insulation performance), and even triple-cell configurations. Roman shades are available in TDBU configurations but are heavier and slightly less smooth in operation. Roller shades are generally not available in TDBU due to the structural requirements of the two-rail system.

- Cellular (honeycomb): Best choice — smooth operation, excellent insulation, wide product range
- Roman shades: Available in TDBU but heavier and less common
- Roller shades: Not available in standard TDBU configuration
- Pleated shades: Available in TDBU, lighter weight alternative to cellular

## Garden Units and First-Floor Chicago Apartments

For Chicago garden apartments — the below-street-level units common in Lakeview, Lincoln Park, and Roscoe Village two-flats — TDBU shades are particularly valuable. These units face an elevated street sightline looking down into the unit, which is even more invasive than standard street-level viewing. A TDBU cellular shade on a garden unit window can be positioned with the bottom rail raised high (blocking the upper portion from the elevated street sightline) while the top of the shade is also lowered slightly to create a mid-window light band — complex privacy geometry that standard shades cannot achieve.

## Motorized TDBU: The Next Level

Manual TDBU shades require two separate cord or cordless operations: one to raise and lower the bottom rail, one to raise and lower the top rail. For most homeowners this is straightforward but slightly more involved than a single-rail shade. Motorized TDBU systems automate both rails independently — you can program the shades to open to a specific privacy-and-daylight configuration at sunrise, and close fully at sunset. ## Measuring for TDBU Shades

TDBU shades require particularly precise measurement because both rails must clear the window frame at all positions. Inside-mount TDBU shades need sufficient window depth to accommodate the slightly larger headrail hardware (typically 2–2.5 inches deep). Most Chicago vintage windows have adequate inside-mount depth for TDBU. Outside-mount TDBU eliminates depth concerns but requires deliberate decisions about how far beyond the frame the shade extends.
