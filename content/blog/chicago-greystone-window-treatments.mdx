Chicago has more greystone buildings than any other city in America — estimates put the count at over 70,000 buildings citywide, concentrated in Lincoln Park, Wicker Park, Logan Square, Pilsen, Bridgeport, and dozens of other neighborhoods. These buildings, built primarily between 1880 and 1930, have distinctive window challenges that require more expertise than standard installation. After outfitting hundreds of greystone properties, here's what we've learned.

## The Classic Chicago Bay Window Problem

The defining architectural feature of Chicago greystone two-flats is the front bay window: three angled panes projecting from the building facade, often spanning the full height of the front room from floor to ceiling. Bay windows require individual treatment of each pane (because the angles prevent a single shade from spanning the full width), coordinated from a design perspective so the bay reads as a unified element, and careful measurement of each pane's inside-mount depth — which often varies across the three openings.

### Our Bay Window Approach

For Chicago greystone bay windows, we typically recommend: inside-mount cellular or roller shades on each of the three panes for light and insulation control, plus a Roman shade or drapery on the center pane if the room calls for a fabric element. The goal is functional light control on every pane while creating a cohesive visual. We avoid trying to fit a single continuous treatment across a bay — it rarely works well mechanically and often looks awkward.

## Original Frames: The Measurement Challenge

Greystone windows from the 1880–1930 era were built to approximate dimensions, not precision standards. A window nominally described as 36 inches wide might measure 35.5 inches at the top, 35.75 at the middle, and 36.25 at the bottom. The inside-mount depth might be 2.75 inches — sufficient for a standard shade headrail — or 1.5 inches in a frame with extra trim that limits your options. We measure width at three heights, height at three widths, and inside-mount depth before specifying any treatment for a greystone opening.

## Original Wavy Glass: Outside Mount Is Your Friend

Many Chicago greystones retain their original cylinder glass — the slightly wavy, irregular glass made by a now-obsolete manufacturing process that gives older windows their distinctive character. This glass is difficult or impossible to replace (a preservation loss the city takes seriously), and it sometimes makes frame integrity uncertain enough that inside-mount treatments are problematic. For windows with original wavy glass, we typically recommend outside-mount roller or cellular shades positioned 1–2 inches above the frame, with sufficient width to cover the frame edges.

## Tall Windows and Floor-to-Ceiling Drama

Chicago greystones typically have 9–11 foot ceilings in their principal rooms, with windows that may run 7–9 feet in height. These tall windows are one of the neighborhood's most appealing architectural features — and they require treatments that capitalize on the height rather than cutting it off visually. Floor-to-ceiling drapery hung from ceiling-height rods (even on windows that don't start at the ceiling) emphasizes the room's vertical dimension. Roman shades on tall windows can look elegant but require careful fabric selection to avoid heavy, institutional-looking folds. Wood blinds on tall windows work beautifully and are one of our most popular greystone recommendations.

## Transom Windows: Often Overlooked

Many Chicago greystones have transom windows above their primary openings — a smaller fixed or operable window above the main sash, separated by a horizontal rail. These are often left untreated, which creates light control and privacy problems. We recommend matching cellular or roller shades on the transom that operate independently from the main window. In many cases a fixed-mount shade on the transom (non-operable) is sufficient and simpler to install.

## Landlord vs. Owner Considerations

Many Chicago greystones are owner-occupied on the first floor with a rental unit above — the classic Chicago two-flat arrangement. Landlords often provide window treatments for rental units; cellular shades are our recommendation for rental applications due to their durability, child safety (cordless), and tenants' appreciation for their insulating and light-blocking properties. For owner-occupied floors, the full range of options is appropriate and we recommend treating the investment level to match the duration of occupancy.
